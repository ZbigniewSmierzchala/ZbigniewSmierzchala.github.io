Using PowerPoint as a "documentation tool"? Avoid these traps!

  • March 23, 2021
  • By Zbigniew Smierzchala

Throughout my professional carrier, I have seen often PowerPoint used as a tool to document and share ideas within the organization. In such cases, authors often put a lot of text, data, and pictures on each slide, forgetting the basic communication principles.

Don’t know what I am talking about? OK, take a look at the (public) strategy paper of an international, large company, starting straight away with the first slide, which looks like that:

Figure 1 Slide example

Source: Source: 20th Mid-Term Management Plan and Medium to Long-Term Outlook, Ricoh,

How long does it take to grasp the thoughts outlined on the slide? Do you get what is the key message of the slide? What does the percentage number next to the country flag mean?

Actually, one can see at least 3 different key takeaways:

  1. Workers accept “the new normal”
  2. DocuWare sales continue to grow
  3. There is a tremendous opportunity for support services for the new normal

In my opinion, it would make it easier for the audience to understand the author's intention, if each key message would be presented on a separate slide.

So, let us say, that we want to recap how the pandemic transformed work. My key takeaway for the investors would be that “the new normal” creates opportunities for (new) services for the company. I would add some (no more as 3!) key proof points (= data) to substantiate my statement. This would result in a slide like that:

Figure 2. Alternative slide to convey just one message with proof points. Emphasis is on the numbers, with the intention to call out the astonishing growth.

Figure 3. Alternative slide, putting the focus on phenomena of the remote work, not so much on the numbers, thus the use of visuals.

Pictures source: unDraw

There are a lot of great communication and PowerPoint courses on LinkedIn Learning and other Online Learning Portals, but if you don’t have time right now and want to improve immediately your skills – I suggest starting with a short YouTube video by Leila Gharani

She explains these 5 key “golden” rules which make sense to keep in mind while working on presentations:

  1. Visualize data
  2. Use White space - don't clutter
  3. Apply instant design ideas (PowerPoint feature)
  4. Use scalable vector graphics
  5. Apply the rule of thirds
